Case Study — Video Production & Visual Design

"An empty room. A media empire."

I walked into an empty room in a corporate office and turned it into a full-service, in-house media studio. It became the engine behind every company-wide broadcast, branded video, podcast, and training asset for 3,000+ employees across 325+ locations.

Role

Director, Communications & TA

Company

Amtel / T-Mobile Authorized Partner

Reach

3,000+ employees, 325+ locations

Duration

2016 — 2022

The Situation

No studio. No team. No playbook.

Amtel was one of the largest T-Mobile authorized partners in the country, growing from 42 to 325+ retail locations. But the company had no centralized communications strategy, no branded content engine, and no way to reach thousands of employees spread across the country in a way that felt personal and human.

Training was inconsistent. Company culture was getting diluted by distance. Leadership had no reliable channel to connect with the frontline. And the employer brand, both externally and internally, didn't match the energy of the people who actually worked there.

That's where I came in.

The Studio

Built from scratch. Literally.

I sourced, designed, and built the studio myself. From selecting equipment and configuring lighting to building the set, branding the backdrop, and establishing production workflows. No agency. No outside production team. Just the vision, the tools, and the willingness to figure it out.

The studio became the hub for all company-wide communications. It wasn't just a room. It was the engine that drove cultural connection across 325+ locations in multiple states.

Flagship Event

Winner's Circle

From small celebration to company-defining event.

Winner's Circle was an annual recognition event that Amtel had been producing for over 5 years before I joined the company in 2016. It had always been a small gathering to celebrate top performers and operational/sales success throughout the year.

In my third year with the company, I was awarded the opportunity to revamp the entire program. I earned that responsibility because of how I executed during our 60-store organic growth with new locations, and how I managed logistics during our first-ever acquisition in New York City.

I took it to another level. Because Amtel still operated like a startup where everyone wears many hats, I was solely in charge of everything: securing funding within budget, negotiating vendor sponsorships, sourcing event swag, selecting the location, planning the award ceremony, designing the trophies, building the agenda, and project managing every detail. I even personally edited the event videos.

Year over year, we watched the attendee count grow as the celebration got bigger and bigger. More importantly, the culture of recognition and competition that Winner's Circle represented was directly connected to the month-over-month revenue targets we were hitting through the roof. When you recruit the right people, train them well, communicate clearly, and celebrate wins publicly, the results follow.

Confidential Access

T-Mobile Dealer Operations Execution Calls.

I was selected to participate in T-Mobile's confidential Dealer Operations Execution Calls and Dealer Readiness Calls. These were restricted-access executive briefings where corporate shared proprietary updates, product launches, strategic initiatives, and embargoed content with a small group of trusted dealer leaders.

After each briefing, I would debrief our senior leadership, then take the raw corporate materials and remix them into something our field teams could actually use. The Galaxy S20 launch materials, for example, came from one of these calls. I turned corporate slide decks into grab-and-go job aids, visual training guides, execution checklists, and timed communication cascades that our 325+ locations could execute on launch day.

The Express Setup System and the Discovery Conversation job aids are examples of this process. Corporate gave us the what. I built the how.
